# Cross‑Tier Harmonic Stability Checks

## Overview
Cross‑Tier Harmonic Stability Checks (CTHSC) are the ecosystem’s diagnostic mechanisms for detecting when resonance across tiers becomes unstable, distorted, or misaligned. While harmonics enable powerful cross‑tier and cross‑domain insight propagation, they can also drift, amplify noise, or create false analogies if not continuously monitored.

CTHSC ensures that resonance remains coherent, safe, and structurally valid as it moves through the ecosystem.

---

## Purpose
Cross‑Tier Harmonic Stability Checks exist to:

- detect unstable or distorted harmonics  
- prevent false analogies from propagating  
- ensure cross‑tier resonance remains structurally valid  
- protect the ecosystem from epistemic drift  
- support tuning and governance decisions  
- maintain long‑term coherence across tiers  

These checks are the ecosystem’s early detection system for resonance instability.

---

## Rationale
Resonance is powerful but fragile.  
As harmonics propagate across tiers, they can:

- amplify noise  
- distort relational structure  
- lose domain grounding  
- overgeneralize  
- trigger cascading misalignment  

Without stability checks, the ecosystem could drift into conceptual incoherence.

CTHSC provides the structural safeguards that keep resonance meaningful.

---

## What Causes Harmonic Instability?

### **1. Over‑Amplification**
The soundboard amplifies a harmonic beyond its structural integrity.

### **2. Cross‑Tier Misalignment**
A harmonic fits Tier 4 and Tier 3 but breaks at Tier 2 or Tier 1.

### **3. False Analogies**
Patterns appear similar but lack deep relational equivalence.

### **4. Noise Contamination**
High‑entropy signals masquerade as meaningful harmonics.

### **5. Abstraction Drift**
The relational core shifts as it migrates downward.

Each of these failure modes requires detection and intervention.

---

## Core Stability Check Mechanisms

### **1. Structural Consistency Verification**
The ecosystem checks whether the harmonic’s relational structure remains intact across tiers.

- Are the core relationships preserved?  
- Does the abstraction remain coherent?  
- Has any tier introduced distortion?  

This is the backbone of stability checking.

---

### **2. Cross‑Tier Resonance Symmetry**
A stable harmonic should resonate similarly across tiers.

The system checks for:

- amplitude symmetry  
- relational symmetry  
- abstraction symmetry  

Asymmetry indicates instability.

---

### **3. Domain‑Grounding Validation**
The system verifies that the harmonic:

- still maps to real domain structures  
- has not drifted into overgeneralization  
- remains interpretable at lower tiers  

Loss of grounding triggers damping or scrubbing.

---

### **4. Noise‑Signal Separation**
The ecosystem evaluates whether the harmonic contains:

- high‑entropy contamination  
- spurious correlations  
- unstable oscillations  

If noise dominates, the harmonic is suppressed.

---

### **5. Propagation Integrity Checks**
The system monitors:

- propagation speed  
- propagation amplitude  
- cross‑tier migration patterns  

Unusual propagation behavior often signals instability.

---

### **6. Harmonic Drift Detection**
The system detects slow, subtle shifts in:

- relational structure  
- abstraction boundaries  
- cross‑domain mapping  

Drift is one of the most dangerous forms of instability.

---

## Example (Abstracted)

**Situation:**  
A new harmonic emerges linking oscillatory patterns across three domains.

**Stability Check Process:**

1. **Structural Consistency:**  
   The relational form is preserved across Tier 4 → Tier 3 → Tier 2.

2. **Symmetry Check:**  
   Amplitude symmetry holds, but Tier 1 shows distortion.

3. **Domain‑Grounding:**  
   Tier 1 cannot map the abstraction to real‑world structures.

4. **Noise‑Signal Separation:**  
   Tier 1’s distortion is due to noise contamination.

5. **Propagation Integrity:**  
   Propagation slows at Tier 1, indicating instability.

**Outcome:**  
The system applies damping, refines the abstraction, and re‑tests stability before allowing further propagation.
